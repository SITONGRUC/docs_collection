\noindent Dear Professor Dube,

\vspace{1em}

I am writing to apply for the Research Professional position at the Becker Friedman
Institute for Economics. My long-term goal is to pursue a Ph.D.\ in economics, and
your research program---on how political and social forces shape conflict, gender
inequality, and development---is precisely where I want to deepen as an empirical
researcher.

Your work speaks to questions I find deeply compelling. The Nigeria field experiment,
studying how contact between warring groups affects farmer-herder violence, is a
striking example of applying a randomized design to a problem where causal
identification is both genuinely difficult and genuinely consequential: standard
observational approaches cannot disentangle whether dialogue reduces violence or
whether peaceful communities are simply more willing to engage. I am excited by the
prospect of contributing to the data pipeline and analysis for this project, whether
in cleaning and validating the experimental data, building outcome measures from
administrative or satellite sources, or implementing the core estimators. The
media-and-gender-norms project draws me in for different reasons. Measuring how
exposure to Western media reshapes attitudes in an Islamic society requires both
creative big-data construction and careful causal identification---exactly the
intersection of skills I have been developing. I am drawn to your use of novel data
sources to study deeply rooted disparities that are resistant to easy measurement,
and I would welcome the opportunity to contribute to any stage of that project.

My path to development economics and political economy runs through two distinct
experiences. Before coming to the United States, I worked as a fixed-income analyst
at Harvest Fund Management, one of China's largest asset managers, covering local
government bonds. That work forced me to think concretely about how political
institutions and fiscal constraints translate into real outcomes for ordinary
people---whether roads get built, whether public services are delivered, and
ultimately how resource allocation under different governance structures shapes
development. The connection between political economy and human welfare is not
abstract to me; I spent years watching it play out in bond prices and infrastructure
investment data. At the same time, I became fascinated by the measurement
challenges involved: how do you identify the causal effect of a governance reform
when political change is never random? That question led me toward empirical
economics, and toward programs like yours that take identification seriously.

Since arriving in the United States three years ago, I have built a broad empirical
toolkit. I am proficient in Python and have constructed reproducible pipelines for
processing large, heterogeneous datasets---including administrative records,
geospatial data, and text corpora. I have hands-on experience implementing
difference-in-differences and event-study designs, including stacked-cohort
specifications, pre-trend diagnostics, and sensitivity analyses. I also have
substantial experience with large language models and modern NLP methods. I
understand that extracting structured information from news archives, social media,
and survey instruments is increasingly central to research in conflict and media
studies, and I have developed workflows---with careful attention to robustness and
reproducibility---for doing so at scale. I am comfortable with Python and R, and I
am actively developing proficiency in Stata. I also have experience with geospatial
analysis, which I understand is relevant to the Nigeria project's geographic scope.

As a Research Professional, I would contribute in three concrete ways. First, I can
build and maintain scalable, reproducible pipelines for the large administrative,
survey, and media datasets both projects require. Second, I can implement and extend
the causal identification strategies at the core of this work---RCT analysis,
difference-in-differences, and related approaches---and write clean code that
collaborators across institutions can audit and build on. Third, I can deploy
computational text and media methods to extract information from news sources,
survey instruments, and online data at scale. I am strongly committed to working for
the full two-year term and am available to begin July 1, 2026.

I am grateful for your consideration and would welcome the chance to discuss how my
background fits your current projects.
